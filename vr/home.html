Evaluation of the state of Avatars

Introduction

Using Facebook's Oculus Quest 2, I evaluated avatars in Spatial, RecRoom, Frame VR, Echo VR, and VR Chat using metrics I researched from Google Scholar. With this information, I recommended multiple ways Spatial could improve its Avatar in Communication, Appearance, Movement, and Performance.

Evaluation Metrics

After reviewing multiple research papers from Google Scholar about designing better avatars, I came up with different domains or criteria that would help determine the state of avatars in each software.

Softwares

For each of the softwares, I contributed to a public wiki by adding tutorials and software overviews, some in collaboration with my classmates.

Spatial

Spatial is a virtual workspace that allows users to create lifelike avatars and work closely together sharing content and collaborating on white boards as if in an actual meeting room. Key findings of avatar evaluations are listed below. You can find an overview of the software here.

  • Emotion of the avatar is not taken into account from the user or voice or gestures.
  • There is a disorientation between the teeth.
  • There is a toggle for Spatial audio that works in a realistic audio volume level.
  • The movement of the joints is bizarre.
  • The avatar doesn't take hijabs into account in the avatar generation algorithm.
  • There are only two body types which doesn't represent a diverse range.
  • The avatar assumes no physical disabilities as it only considers the user’s face when creating the avatar.
  • Movement in Spatial is not freeform, but rather restricted to small steps.
  • Spatial's Supported Headsets: the Microsoft HoloLens 1, Hololens 2, Oculus Quest, Quest 2 , Nreal and Magic Leap headsets.

RecRoom

RecRoom is a virtual space to build and play games together with your friends from all around the world. Key findings of avatar evaluations are listed below. You can find an overview of the software here.

  • Recroom uses the user's audio output, body language, proximity to other people, objects they're holding and using, and the things that are happening to and around them to "create" emotions.
  • Avatars can voice and text chat with their friends and send invites to games/events.
  • Users can Mute or Ignore. There is also a talk to the hand feature which is quite literal.
  • The avatar can walk/teleport and jump/fly whihc alternatives for sickness.
  • The avatar supports different clothing and appearance with diversity in color.
  • The avatar doesn’t represent people with physical disabilities.
  • The avatar doesn’t have distinguishable fingers besides the thumb (and index finger when invoked) in its rubber hand.
  • Users found it difficult to customize the avatar and learn controls.

FrameVR

FrameVR is a 3D collaboration and creation tool where users can invite others to join their workspaces for a meeting. Key findings of avatar evaluations are listed below. You can find an overview of the software here.

  • The facial expressions of the user are NOT mimicked in the avatar when the avatar cam is turned on.
  • Avatars don’t have a physical interaction. Since frames are invite only, harassment can be avoided.
  • Avatars can talk to other avatars through voice and text chat in Frame.
  • The avatar has no limbs/fingers. There is no lip syncing, not even in the level of RecRoom.
  • Customization for the torso of the avatar including, skin tone, eye color, hair color, hair/hat style, facial hair, glasses, and earrings.
  • The user’s mood is not expressed in any form through the avatar.

Echo VR

Echo VR is a multiplayer gaming software in zero gravity atmosphere available to all Oculus users. Key findings of avatar evaluations are listed below. You can find an overview here.

  • Users can set the emotes or voice pitch/module while customizing.
  • There is a huge arena in the lobby so players can only hear each other well enough if they’re in the same room/part of the lobby.
  • Avatars can grab objects and thrust forward.
  • There is gaze directed travel through thrust boosters and on the Y button on the controller.
  • The avatar vibrates when the it dashes or grabs an object and when its head gets punched.
  • The avatar doesn’t have lips or control over the lower body. However the movement of each joint in the arms is top notch.
  • Users can change the tint, pattern, as well as decals on various parts of the robotic body.
  • There is extreme motion sickness with boosters.

VR Chat

  • The avatar has emotes it can display like wave, clap, point, etc.
  • The avatar can send emojis to express any feeling.
  • The avatar can sit or stand. It can also move in third person mode for users with motion sickness.
  • Avatars come with lip sync, and complete range of motion.
  • The avatar supports eye tracking/blinking. It also supports gaze directed travel.
  • The user has a wide range of avatars to choose from in the options (doesn't even have to be human). They can also create their own avatar.
  • The simulation sickness is not as bad as EchoVR but is worse than RecRoom.

In Class Activity

To compare different softwares, I organized a class activity to focus on the appearance and movement of avatars in RecRoom and Spatial. The main task was to observe bowling in RecRoom and note taking in Spatial for movement along with customization options for appearance.

The results of the class activity were included in my recommendations to Spatial. Most of the questionnaire was qualitative
